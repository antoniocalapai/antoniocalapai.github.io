Neuronal Encoding of Motion and Depth

As part of my electrophysiology work, I investigated how single neurons in the macaque Middle Superior Temporal area (MST) encode motion and depth information. Monkeys were trained to fixate a central point, press a button for a dimming target, and wear polarised glasses enabling presentation of stimuli at different depths.

During the task, I recorded extracellular activity while clouds of moving dots varied rapidly in motion direction and binocular disparity. This allowed me to map receptive fields and quantify joint tuning to motion and depth using Reverse Correlation.

Theory suggested that some MST neurons might act as self-motion detectors, responding to specific combinations of direction and depth. In practice, I found a diversity of tuning profiles:

  • specific motion–depth combinations,
  • motion-only tuning,
  • depth-only tuning,
  • mixed or complex patterns.

Using Generalised Additive Models (GAMs) on 194 neurons, only one neuron showed tuning consistent with a true self-motion detector. Most neurons appeared to provide intermediate building blocks for self-motion processing, but not the complete computation itself.

These findings indicate that MST does not explicitly encode self-motion. Instead, it provides rich motion–depth representations that are likely combined downstream to support perception of self-movement through space.
